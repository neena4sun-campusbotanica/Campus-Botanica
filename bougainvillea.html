BOUGAINVILLEA

Basic Information

Common Name: Bougainvillea
Scientific Name: Bougainvillea spectabilis
Family: Nyctaginaceae
Origin: South America, especially Brazil
Plant Type: Thorny ornamental vine and shrub

Identification Features

Bougainvillea is known for its brightly colored
bracts that surround small white flowers.
The plant has woody stems, sharp thorns and green oval leaves.

Growing Conditions

-Requires full sunlight for best flowering.
-Grows well in well-drained soil.
-Needs moderate watering and warm temperatures.

Benefits
  • Beautiful ornamental plant.
  • Used for hedges and fencing.
  • Attracts butterflies and pollinators.

Common Problems
  • Aphid infestation.
  • Root rot due to overwatering.
  • Poor flowering because of insufficient sunlight.

Propagation

Usually propagated through stem cuttings during the growing season.

Care Tips
  • Provide at least 6 hours of sunlight.
  • Avoid excessive watering.
  • Prune regularly to maintain shape.

Interesting Facts
  • The colorful parts are modified leaves called bracts.
  • It is named after French explorer Louis Antoine de Bougainville.
  • Can bloom almost year-round in tropical climates.
Blooming period

Its primary growing season runs from spring through early fall, roughly from March to October in tropical cycles.

  • Peak Growth & Flowering: The plant shows its most prolific flowering during the hottest months—typically April to June and again in early autumn (September to October).
  • Active Growing Phase: The broader growing season runs from March through October. During this time, the plant puts on significant vegetative growth, requiring monthly or bi-weekly fertilization to support healthy foliage and blooming.
    • Winter Dormancy: Growth slows down considerably or halts entirely during the cooler winter months (November to February). In tropical or Mediterranean climates, they may still bloom, but in cooler zones, they become dormant or require indoor protection to survive temperatures below 40°F (4°C).

    They are native to the tropical forests of South America (specifically Brazil, Bolivia, Paraguay, Peru, Uruguay and Argentina). There are between 4 and 22 species in the genus.
